Chronic Achilles Tendinopathy Read as a Blood Stasis Pattern

A chronic Achilles pain that has become sharp, fixed, and stabbing — worse at rest and after old, incompletely settled strain — points to Blood stasis: congealed, non-moving Blood obstructing the tendon, a distinct configuration from an under-nourished one.

Step 1 · Presentation

The Presenting Picture

Consider a composite presentation seen repeatedly in clinic: an adult with long-standing pain and thickening at the Achilles tendon, following one or more old strains that never fully settled. The pain has taken on a sharp, stabbing, pinpoint quality, fixed in one spot, sometimes worse at rest or first thing rather than only with use, with local thickening or a dusky, congested look to the tissue.

Beyond the chief complaint, a cluster of accompanying signs recurs in this pattern:

  • Sharp, stabbing, pinpoint pain fixed in one location — not a diffuse ache
  • A long history of old, incompletely settled strain to the same tendon
  • Local thickening, nodularity, or a dusky, congested quality to the tissue
  • Pain that can be worse at rest or on first movement, not only with loading

The signatures of Blood stasis are specific: fixed location, stabbing quality, chronicity, a history of old injury, and dusky, thickened tissue. Together they distinguish this from a fresh inflammation or a simple under-nourishment of the tendon.

Step 2 · Constitutional Reading

Reading the Pattern: Blood Stasis (瘀血)

Blood stasis describes Blood that has stopped moving and congealed in the tissue — here, in a tendon repeatedly strained and never fully cleared. The stagnant Blood obstructs local circulation, and where Blood does not move, the pain is fixed and stabbing rather than dull and shifting. The thickening and dusky quality are the visible marks of the congestion.

On the four axes this reads as a local Excess obstruction — a tangible stasis is present — of a Blood, rather than Qi or Damp, character. This is a different obstruction from the Cold-Damp Bi shoulder (003, external pathogen) and a different problem entirely from the Liver Blood-deficient fascia (007, a shortage of nourishment). Stasis is congealed presence; deficiency is absence.

A fixed, stabbing, old-injury pain is congealed Blood that has stopped moving — so the direction is to break the stasis and restore circulation, not to nourish or to warm.

Step 3 · Diagnostic Logic

Why This Reading — and Not Another

The key differential is against a deficiency tendinopathy (case 007): dull, under-nourished, dry, cramping, relieved by rest, tied to depletion. That calls for nourishing Blood to feed the sinew. Blood stasis is the opposite configuration — a fixed, stabbing, congested obstruction from old injury — calling for moving and breaking up the stasis. Nourishing a stasis, or moving a deficiency, would each go the wrong way.

It also differs from an acute, hot inflammation (Damp-Heat, as in the elbow case 006), which is red, hot, and recent rather than chronic, fixed, and dusky. The chronicity, the fixed stabbing quality, and the old-injury history are what mark this specifically as stasis.

For this Blood stasis reading, the treatment direction is to invigorate Blood, break up the local stasis, and restore circulation through the tendon, generally alongside graded loading. This describes a direction of treatment, not a guaranteed outcome; responses vary by individual.
