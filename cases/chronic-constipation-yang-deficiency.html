Elderly Constipation Read as a Yang Deficiency (Cold) Pattern

Not all constipation is heat and dryness. In an older adult with cold signs and low energy, sluggish bowels are read as a Yang deficiency that cannot warm and propel — a reading opposite to the heat-type, offered as education alongside medical care.

Step 1 · Presentation

The Presenting Picture

Consider a composite presentation seen repeatedly in clinic: an older adult with chronic constipation that is difficult and sluggish rather than dry-and-hard, with a sense of no propulsive force, alongside cold feet, low energy, a pale tongue, and a preference for warmth. This cold, powerless quality is the signature the reading works from.

Beyond the chief complaint, a cluster of accompanying signs recurs in this pattern:

  • Sluggish, difficult bowel movements with a sense of weak propulsion
  • Cold feet and a general preference for warmth
  • Low energy and fatigue rather than heat or restlessness
  • Stool that is not necessarily dry-and-hard — the problem is drive, not just dryness

Constipation is assessed medically. The classical framework distinguishes its type: this cold, low-energy, non-propulsive picture points not to heat-dryness but to a Yang deficiency that cannot warm and move the bowel.

Step 2 · Constitutional Reading

Reading the Pattern: Yang Deficiency — Cold Constipation (冷秘)

Classically, Yang provides the warmth and motive force for transportation, including of the bowels. When Yang (Spleen and Kidney) is deficient — common with age — the intestines lose warmth and propulsive drive, and stool moves sluggishly. This “cold constipation” (冷秘) is a deficiency of drive, not an excess of heat.

On the four axes this reads as Interior, Cold, Deficient — the opposite thermal reading from a heat-dryness constipation. Classical Medicine and Modern Disease differentiates the constipation types and reads the elderly cold type through Yang deficiency.

Cold, powerless bowels are Yang that cannot warm and propel — so the direction is to warm and tonify Yang, the opposite of draining a heat-type, alongside medical care.

Step 3 · Diagnostic Logic

Why This Reading — and Not Another

The key differential is thermal and by mechanism: a heat-dryness constipation is hot, dry, and hard, calling for moistening and clearing; a Qi-stagnation constipation is tied to tension and distension; this Yang-deficiency type is cold and non-propulsive, calling for warming and tonifying. Draining a cold-deficiency type, or warming a heat type, would each go the wrong way.

Constipation can have serious causes and is medically evaluated, especially when new or changing. This case is strictly educational and supportive: it does not replace medical evaluation or care and is not a reason to ignore warning signs. It describes acupuncture considered alongside medical care.

For this Yang deficiency reading, the direction is to warm and tonify Yang to restore propulsive warmth to the bowel, as supportive care alongside medical management and appropriate diet. This describes a direction of treatment, not a guaranteed outcome; responses vary by individual, and medical care should continue as directed.
