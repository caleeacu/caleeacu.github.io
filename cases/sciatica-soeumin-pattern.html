Chronic Sciatica Read Through Constitutional Diagnosis

How a presentation of chronic sciatic pain, unresponsive to standard physical therapy, is interpreted through Sasang constitutional reasoning — and why the same symptom can call for different treatment directions in different constitutions.

Pain & Musculoskeletal Sasang: Soeumin (少陰人) Keyword: sciatica constitutional diagnosis

Step 1 · Presentation

The Presenting Picture

Consider a composite presentation seen repeatedly in clinic: an adult in their 50s with unilateral pain radiating from the buttock down the posterior thigh, persisting for more than six months. Imaging shows a mild lumbar disc bulge — findings that do not fully account for the severity or persistence of the pain. A structured course of physical therapy produced early improvement, then plateaued.

Beyond the chief complaint, a cluster of accompanying signs recurs in this pattern:

  • Pain noticeably worse in cold weather and after prolonged sitting on cold surfaces
  • Persistently cold hands and feet, with a general preference for warmth
  • Weak digestion — easily bloated, low appetite in the morning, aversion to cold foods
  • Fatigue that is heaviest on waking and improves somewhat with gentle activity
  • A quiet, inward temperament; symptoms are described precisely but understated

In a disease-centered framework, these accompanying signs are largely noise around the diagnosis of "sciatica." In Korean acupuncture, they are the primary diagnostic data.

Step 2 · Constitutional Reading

Reading the Pattern: Soeumin (少陰人)

Within Sasang constitutional medicine, this cluster — cold-predominance, weak digestive fire, morning-heavy fatigue, cold-aggravated pain, and an inward temperament — coheres around the Soeumin constitutional tendency: a baseline in which the digestive Yang is constitutionally modest and the body's warming, transporting functions are easily depleted.

On the four diagnostic axes of Eight Principle Pattern Identification, the reading resolves as predominantly Interior (the process is rooted in internal organ function, not a recent external invasion), Cold (slowed, under-functioning quality; cold aggravation; preference for warmth), and Deficient (insufficiency of righteous Qi rather than accumulation of a pathogenic excess) — integrating into an overall Yin-directed pattern.

The sciatic pain is read not as an isolated structural event, but as the local expression of a constitution-wide cold-deficient configuration — which is why treating the leg alone plateaued.

Step 3 · Diagnostic Logic

Why This Reading — and Not Another

The decisive question is differential: the identical chief complaint of sciatica can arise within very different constitutional configurations, and each calls for a different treatment direction.

Had the accompanying signs pointed the other way — heat aversion, robust appetite, a loud and expansive presentation, pain aggravated by heat and pressure rather than cold, a full and forceful pulse — the same "sciatica" would be read as an excess-heat configuration, more typical of a Taeeumin or Soyangin presentation, and the treatment direction would reverse: draining and cooling rather than warming and tonifying.

This is the framework's central claim in miniature: the same symptom may indicate different patterns depending on constitutional context and accompanying signs. Diagnosis here is not symptom-to-treatment matching but an interpretive inquiry — which relational imbalance is currently expressing itself through this pain?

For this Soeumin cold-deficient reading, the treatment direction is to warm and restore the constitutionally weak digestive Yang while addressing the meridian pathway of the pain — supporting the systemic root so that the local symptom has a foundation from which to resolve. This describes a direction of treatment, not a guaranteed outcome; responses vary by individual.
