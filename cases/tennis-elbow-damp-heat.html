Clinical Case Library · Case 006

Tennis Elbow Read as a Local Damp-Heat Pattern

Lateral elbow pain that feels hot, tender, and inflamed to the touch points toward a Damp-Heat reading — a configuration whose treatment direction is the near-opposite of the cold, warming approach used for a cold-type joint.

Step 1 · Presentation

The Presenting Picture

Consider a composite presentation seen repeatedly in clinic: an adult with pain over the outer elbow that developed after a period of repetitive gripping or loading, now tender to touch, sometimes warm or slightly swollen, and aggravated by use. Unlike a cold, aching joint, this elbow feels hot and irritable, and cold applications feel better than heat.

Beyond the chief complaint, a cluster of accompanying signs recurs in this pattern:

  • Pain and tenderness localized to the outer elbow, often after repetitive strain
  • A hot, inflamed quality — tender to touch, sometimes warm or mildly swollen
  • Relief from cold applications and aggravation from heat — the reverse of a cold pattern
  • Worse with continued use and gripping; the local tissue feels irritable rather than weak

The signature to notice is thermal. A joint problem that feels hot and prefers cold is telling you something specific about the configuration — and it points in the opposite direction from the cold, damp shoulder in case 003, even though both are overuse-related joint pains.

Step 2 · Constitutional Reading

Reading the Pattern: Local Damp-Heat (濕熱)

This reads as a local Damp-Heat pattern. Repetitive strain generates local stagnation; where that stagnation takes on a hot, inflamed, slightly swollen quality, Heat has combined with Damp in the channels around the joint. The tenderness, warmth, and preference for cold are the defining Heat signs; the swelling and lingering quality are the Damp signs.

On the four axes this is an Excess pattern (an accumulation — Damp-Heat — is present, obstructing the channel) with a clear Heat reading. This Heat/Excess signature is what sets it apart within the pain category: cases 002 (cold, deficient) and 003 (cold, damp) sit at the cold end of the Cold/Heat axis, while this case sits firmly at the hot end.

Two overuse elbows can call for opposite directions: one warmed, one cooled — the thermal quality of the local tissue is what decides.

Step 3 · Diagnostic Logic

Why This Reading — and Not Another

The differential is against a cold or deficient tendinopathy, where the same lateral elbow pain would be pale rather than red, relieved by warmth rather than cold, and weak rather than irritable. There the direction would be to warm and tonify — exactly what would aggravate a Damp-Heat elbow.

This is why a single “tennis elbow protocol” is inadequate: the meridian and the anatomy are the same, but the configuration — hot-excess versus cold-deficient — dictates whether the treatment cools and drains or warms and supports. Misreading the thermal quality sends the whole approach the wrong way.

For this Damp-Heat reading, the treatment direction is to clear Heat, drain Damp, and free the local channel obstruction, generally alongside relative rest from the aggravating load. This describes a direction of treatment, not a guaranteed outcome; responses vary by individual.
